# Expected Q&A

Rank-ordered, most-likely first. Short answer for verbal response, detail if pushed.

---

## Q1. So does opponent modeling help?

**Short**: Not uniformly — it shifts equilibrium. Pred loses 8 return points, prey gains 11. **OA-IQL makes prey better, at the cost of pred.**

**Detail**: Opponent modeling adds information to the shared trunk. For prey, that information is "which of 3 predators is about to charge me" (3 × 5 = 15 outputs per step). For pred, it's "which single move will the prey make" (5 outputs). Prey has more to gain from opponent modeling because the information content is higher. In a zero-sum-like setting, whoever's opponent model is more informative pulls the equilibrium their way. This also shows up in early training: pred *peaks higher* early (+144 vs +103), then loses that lead as prey catches up.

---

## Q2. Is that a good result?

**Short**: It's a more interesting result than a clean win. Adversarial settings are rarely "add method X to both sides → both improve." The asymmetric impact is the *story*.

**Detail**: A uniform improvement in a zero-sum game would be suspicious — it would mean you found a Pareto-improvement in a space where none should exist. An equilibrium shift is the typical honest outcome. If you want OA-IQL to benefit pred, you'd asymmetrize: larger `OPP_AUX_COEF` on the pred side, smaller on prey. That's a one-line knob change.

---

## Q3. Why not just train pred with OA, leave prey baseline?

**Short**: That's the right next experiment if you want pred to benefit. We can launch it — add an `OPP_AUX_COEF_PRED` / `_PREY` split and run.

**Detail**: In the current code, both teams get OA at the same strength (0.5). For an asymmetric benefit: pred with OA vs baseline prey should make pred stronger (prey has no opponent model to exploit). The co-training literature calls this a "exploiter" study.

---

## Q4. What's the opponent head doing architecturally?

**Short**: Extra Dense(64 → opp_n × 5) after the GRU. Outputs logits over opponent actions. Trained with cross-entropy loss at 0.5× weight. Discarded at eval.

**Detail**: See `src/iql_teams_oa.py:71-95`. The key design choice is *shared trunk*: both the Q-head and the opp-head read off the same GRU output, so the opp-loss gradient flows into the trunk and shapes the representation the Q-head sees. If you instead used a *separate* trunk for the opp-head, you'd get opp-prediction accuracy but no policy benefit.

---

## Q5. Why 0.5 for `OPP_AUX_COEF`?

**Short**: First try. Not tuned. At 0.0 it reduces to baseline (exactly). Higher values over-weight the auxiliary at the expense of Q-learning.

**Detail**: Standard aux-loss weights in representation-learning literature (UNREAL, CURL) are 0.05–1.0. 0.5 was my prior. A sensible sweep: {0.1, 0.25, 0.5, 1.0}. Each run is ~2.5 min, so the sweep is ~10 min.

---

## Q6. Is opp-action accuracy a good training signal?

**Short**: Yes, as a diagnostic. It climbs from 20 % (random) to ~57 % and mirrors the Q-learning phase transition. Flat accuracy = trunk is not learning opponent dynamics.

**Detail**: 57 % accuracy at ε = 0.05 is significant — the opponent has 5 options and still acts with 5 % randomness, so the ceiling is around 0.95. But much of the prey's policy is consistent (go up-right when pred is bottom-left), which is what the head is capturing.

---

## Q7. Why does pred peak higher with OA-IQL?

**Short**: Sample efficiency. OA pred's Q-values are conditioned on a trunk that already encodes "where is the prey likely to be" from step 1 of training, so early exploration converts to useful Q-estimates faster.

**Detail**: Baseline pred has to learn that trunk representation implicitly from the Q-loss gradient alone. OA pred gets a dense, stable gradient from opp-CE — much easier signal than the sparse Bellman backup. That's standard representation-learning lore (see Hinton on auxiliary tasks).

---

## Q8. Why does OA pred *lose* the lead eventually?

**Short**: Because OA prey is also benefiting — more so. Prey closes the information gap faster than pred can open it. Equilibrium shifts.

**Detail**: It's the co-training non-stationarity bite. Any policy improvement is measured relative to the opposing policy, which is also improving. A method that helps both sides can still hurt one side if it helps the other side more.

---

## Q9. 1 seed — what's the variance?

**Short**: Each run is 2.5 min. Multi-seed is a flag change: `NUM_SEEDS: 5` already supported via `jax.vmap`. Happy to launch.

**Detail**: Wall-clock for `NUM_SEEDS=5` is roughly 5× due to CPU (no GPU parallelism), so ~12–15 min total. Budget-safe before any write-up.

---

## Q10. Why γ = 0.9?

**Short**: 25-step episodes, so γ²⁵ ≈ 0.07 — rewards beyond episode end are already discounted out. γ = 0.99 would weight phantom post-episode rewards.

---

## Q11. What's the cost?

**Short**: 5 % in wall-clock (150 → 158 s), 5 % in throughput, ~50 extra parameters per team's Dense layer. The aux head is ~5 % of the Q-net's param count.

---

## Q12. What happens if I scale `OPP_AUX_COEF` to 10?

**Short**: The trunk starts optimizing for opp-prediction at the expense of Q-values. Q-loss would stay high; opp-accuracy would climb but returns would tank. Degenerate aux-task dominance.

---

## Q13. What if the prey's policy is nearly random?

**Short**: Then opp-head accuracy plateaus near 0.20 and the auxiliary loss approaches entropy of uniform — no signal, no harm. OA-IQL reduces gracefully to baseline in that limit.

---

## Q14. Why not predict opponent *rewards* instead of actions?

**Short**: Actions are in the buffer, free. Rewards for the opponent are also in the buffer (`rewards` is a dict), so you could do both. Rewards carry less bit-rate — it's usually sparse — but they're also more policy-invariant.

**Detail**: This is exactly the BIRL / inverse-RL direction. Predicting opp rewards approximates the opponent's latent objective, which is invariant under policy changes as long as the goal is stable. Predicting opp actions gives you a classifier that tracks the current policy. Different trade-offs. OA-IQL is the cheaper side; an IRL head is the heavier side.

---

## Q15. Is this a planner?

**Short**: No. OA-IQL is still model-free Q-learning — no tree search, no explicit rollouts. The opp-head is a *representation-shaping auxiliary*, not a planning primitive.

**Detail**: You could use the opp-head as a 1-step opponent predictor inside a lookahead (Q(s,a) averaged over predicted opp-moves), or as a leaf-value estimator in MCTS. Neither is in this repo. The point here is the cheap drop-in version.

---

## Q16. What's the PettingZoo convention in obs?

**Short**: Pred obs is 16-dim, prey obs is 14-dim. Pred sees prey's velocity; prey doesn't see pred's. `CTRolloutManager` pads both to 16 and adds a 4-dim agent-ID one-hot → 20 uniform.

---

## Q17. What's the obvious next experiment?

**Short**: Asymmetric `OPP_AUX_COEF` (pred strong, prey weak) — closes the equilibrium gap. Or swap the aux task to opponent-reward prediction (approaching BIRL).

**Detail**: For real lift on the pred side, combine asymmetric aux coef with opp-head-as-lookahead: at action-selection, use opp-head's top-k predictions to evaluate `Q(s, a, opp_pred)` and pick the argmax. 1-step planning with learned opponent model.
